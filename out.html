Although New Orleans Saints' offense put up a whopping 577 yards in their 49-24 victory over New York Giants on Monday night, game's biggest play came courtesy of a very unlikely suspect: backup linebacker Will Herring.

Herring made a terrific interception in end zone to snuff out Giants' first drive when game was scoreless. New York tight end Jake Ballard had a step on Herring, but quarterback Eli Manning didn't have enough steam on his pass, and Herring reached up to snag it.

It was just sixth interception and 10th takeaway of season for Saints' defense, which entered game ranked last in NFL in both categories. Later in game, Saints cornerback Tracy Porter forced a fumble that was recovered by safety Roman Harper. Saints also forced three other fumbles that were recovered by Giants.

Herring's play made biggest impact when momentum was still up for grabs. Saints' opening drive had ended with a deflating fake field-goal attempt that fell 2 yards short. Then Giants nearly marched 90 yards for a quick touchdown before Herring got in way.

It was a huge moment for fifth-year veteran, who hadn't done much since signing with Saints as a free agent from Seattle in July. Herring started at strongside linebacker in Week 1 but fell behind Scott Shanle, Jo-Lonn Dunbar and Jonathan Casillas in pecking order at outside linebacker. Then he missed five games with a hamstring injury.

"You know, that's football, " Herring said of dealing with injury. "I've been thankful coaching staff has stuck with me. Joe Vitt has been encouraging, telling me just to keep my head up and keep preparing."

A week after Saints allowed six sacks at St. Louis last month, guard Carl Nicks said Saints had given up so many sacks that day that they couldn't afford to give up another one for rest of year.

Who knew he was being literal?

For third straight game, Saints didn't allow a sack as offensive line continues to try to erase haunting memory from that St. Louis game during which they stunk up joint both with their pass protection and run blocking.

Ever since that game, I've been saying that Saints will only go as far as their offensive line leads them. They were too inconsistent last season and during first half of this season, especially considering they have what is considered best guard tandem in NFL in Pro Bowlers Nicks and Jahri Evans.

Now it looks like these guys are on a mission. They were terrific Monday night on passing plays against a vaunted Giants defensive line that began week tied for first in NFL with 31 sacks. line was also great on running plays (6.8 yards per carry) and in screen game, which was highlighted by a classic 24-yard gain by tailback Pierre Thomas in third quarter.

HOW GAME WAS WON

Saints were spectacular on offense Monday and are on pace for 7,194 yards this season, which would break NFL record of 7,075 set by St. Louis Rams in 2000. Quarterback Drew Brees, who threw for 363 yards and four touchdowns, is on pace to shatter Dan Marino's record of 5,084 yards, set in 1984. Brees is on pace for 5,366.

This game was best evidence to date that NBC analyst and former Indianapolis Colts Coach Tony Dungy was correct when he told me Sunday that Saints can be Super Bowl contenders because their offense is even better now than it was in 2009, because of emergence of tight end Jimmy Graham and tailback Darren Sproles.

"I really think they can go a long way, " Dungy said of 8-3 Saints, who are third in NFC behind Green Bay Packers (11-0) and San Francisco 49ers (9-2). "I was a little concerned about their defense early in year, but I think they're better off now. And that offense has such a quick-strike capability that any game they play, you can't count them out. Everybody's looking at Green Bay and San Francisco, I think because of their records. But New Orleans has ability to win a game against anybody they play."

Saints' rushing game was also terrific Monday. They ran for 205 yards on 30 carries, including touchdown runs by Thomas, Mark Ingram and Brees.

By way, I know that will lead some fans to point out how well Saints do when they run ball more. But I can't resist noting that Saints were still a pass-first team Monday. By time they jumped to a 35-10 lead in third quarter, they had thrown ball 33 times for 359 yards and run ball 17 times for 102 yards. That's a perfect balance for way they play offense. They don't need to run ball more often -- just more efficiently.
